---
title: "Better ways to learn than looking at pictures of bedrooms"
pubDate: 2016-10-09
tags: ["Close readings of important works"]
---

An [article on Upworthy](http://www.upworthy.com/these-21-bedrooms-from-across-the-world-will-help-you-understand-millennials?c=ufb1) recently displayed some very cool pictures of young people’s bedrooms from around the world. Accompanying these compelling photographs was some absolutely mind numbing text. For example, the article asked the following question:

> “What better way to find out [about the connection between internet connectivity and things like education, women’s rights, and poverty] than to look at people’s bedrooms?”

In order to quell the mind numbing rage that I feel stemming from the article, I will take this question literally, and provide several better ways than looking at pictures of people’s bedrooms to find out just about anything.

1. Asking people questions [about their internet connectivity and things like education, women’s rights, and poverty].
2. Conduct a survey [about internet connectivity and things like education, women’s rights, and poverty].
3. Gather available data [on internet connectivity and things like education, women’s rights, and poverty].
4. Run statistical analysis over available data [on internet connectivity and things like education, women’s rights, and poverty].
5. Think quietly about the possible [connection between internet connectivity and things like education, women’s rights, and poverty].
6. Talk to experts in fields like sociology, anthropology, psychology, history, and economics, etc., about the possible [connection between internet connectivity and things like education, women’s rights, and poverty].
7. Read any of the numerous studies that have been published about [the connection between [internet connectivity](http://www.washington.edu/news/2013/07/10/global-study-stresses-importance-of-public-internet-access/) and things like [education](http://www.pewglobal.org/2015/03/19/internet-seen-as-positive-influence-on-education-but-negative-influence-on-morality-in-emerging-and-developing-nations/), [women’s rights](https://web.archive.org/web/20180902070115/http://webfoundation.org/about/research/womens-rights-online-2015/), and [poverty](http://journalistsresource.org/studies/society/internet/computer-usage-access-low-income-urban-communities)].
